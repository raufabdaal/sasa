## Context

When CBC ICT practicals launched, every candidate was expected to demonstrate skills on a working machine. The reality on the ground was different: as of 2024, **41% of UPE secondary schools** did not have enough working computers for the cohort sitting practicals. Schools improvised. some borrowed lab time, some used phones, some skipped the practical entirely. Marking was inconsistent. Equity suffered.

## The Change

UNEB now formally recognizes **two equivalent assessment paths** for ICT practicals:

- **(a) Live machine**. as before, candidate completes tasks on a working computer.
- **(b) Paper-and-walkthrough**. candidate diagrams the interface and narrates each step in writing, with optional screen-sketches.

**Both are marked against the same construct rubric.** Neither is "easier". the paper path tests deeper articulation; the live path tests deeper fluency. Schools choose based on resources.

## Why

The 2024 access audit. CBC promised competency for *every* learner. A practical exam a candidate cannot sit because their school has no electricity that day is not a measure of competency. it is a measure of geography.

## Classroom Example

Two S4 learners answer the same task: *"Produce a bar chart of the term-1 top 5 performers."* One uses LibreOffice; the other sketches the spreadsheet on paper and narrates each click. Both can score `A` if their construct grades are equally strong.

## Common Pitfall

**Do not assume the paper path is "the easy one."** Examiners are explicitly told to grade Skill and Application as rigorously on paper as on screen. and to flag responses that simply *describe* without *sequencing*. A walkthrough that doesn't show ordered steps fails Skill, on paper just as on machine.
