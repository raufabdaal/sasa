## Context

The 2020 H&PE syllabus split Uganda's 1850-1970 story into two topics. **Colonial Rule** and **The Struggle for Independence**. with a clean break in 1939. Pedagogically, it created a problem: learners memorized two timelines but rarely saw the through-line. The 2024 NCDC review found that S4 learners could list resistance leaders but struggled to explain *why* resistance arose at all.

## The Change

The two topics merge into a **single 14-week thematic arc**: *Power, Resistance, Identity*. The arc spans pre-colonial governance structures, colonial imposition, resistance movements, the path to independence, and post-independence identity formation. taught and assessed as one continuous argument.

## Why

CBC asks for *competency*, not coverage. A learner who can articulate how power was negotiated across 120 years of Ugandan history demonstrates analytical competency. A learner who lists facts in two boxes does not. The merge prioritizes the through-line over the inventory.

## Classroom Example

Lesson 1 of the new arc opens not with 1894 but with the Lukiko in pre-colonial Buganda. *what was it, how did it constrain the Kabaka, why does that matter today?* By week 3, learners trace how British indirect rule reshaped that same institution. By week 12, they are debating federalism in present-day Uganda using the same conceptual vocabulary.

## Common Pitfall

**Don't try to teach the merged topic as "Topic 6 then Topic 7 with a transition slide."** The arc is structured by *theme* (power, resistance, identity) cycling forward through time, not by chronological coverage. Plan your unit around the three concepts; let the dates serve the theme.
