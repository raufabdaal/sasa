The curriculum changed. We help teachers catch up.

Sasa turns every NCDC circular and UNEB update into a 7-minute lesson a Ugandan teacher can teach tomorrow morning.

01 — The Problem

The cascade didn't reach the classroom.

In 2020, Uganda rolled out a Competency-Based Curriculum for lower secondary. It was the right move — and it was rolled out the wrong way. NCDC trained 35% of teachers as "master trainers" and asked them to train the rest. They didn't.

A 2024 UNEB study found most teachers were still setting old-curriculum questions weeks before the first pioneer exam. UNEB only arrived to train teachers on assessment a few months before sitting day. Now, in 2026, the same cascade is being used for A-Level.

The information from NCDC and UNEB keeps changing — construct weights, sample papers, grading letters, new Activities of Integration. There is no single place a teacher can go to find what changed since last term, what it means for Monday, and how to teach it. Sasa is that place.

02 — The Product

Four quiet pillars.

One platform, four screens, one job: keep every teacher current with the curriculum, every term, in under an hour a week.

01 · FEED
What's new?

A calm chronological list of curriculum updates filtered to the subjects you teach. One card, one change. No noise.

02 · BRIEF
What does it mean?

Every card opens a 7-minute structured brief — Context, The Change, Why, Classroom Example, Common Pitfall. With audio.

03 · SANDBOX
Can I do it?

Apply the change in a guided simulation. Re-grade a sample answer. Re-write an old question. AI feedback, citation first.

04 · LEDGER
Am I keeping up?

A quiet record of every update internalized. Exportable as a serif-typeset Retooling Passport you can hand to your head teacher.

03 — The Feed

The morning newspaper, for the new curriculum.

Cards separated by hairlines, not gaps. Subject pill in mono. Title in serif. Two-line summary. Tap once.

04 — The Brief

Seven minutes. Five sections. One change.

Serif body, generous rhythm, sticky section labels as you scroll. The before-and-after diff animates in once, the moment you understand.

CONTEXT 7 MIN · ◐
Chemistry · S3
Construct weights revised for O-Level Chemistry
Effective July 2026 · NCDC Circular 2026/03 · Reviewed by Aisha Namaganda
Context

When the Lower Secondary Curriculum launched in 2020, each Chemistry assessment was weighted 40% Knowledge, 30% Skill, and 30% Application of Knowledge. Values were observed but not separately graded. The intention was to ease teachers and learners into the new construct-based language.

The Change

From the July 2026 term, the weights are redistributed:

Before · 2020 Knowledge 40%
Skill 30%
Application 30%
Values (observed)
After · July 2026 Knowledge 30%
Skill 30%
Application 30%
Values 10%
Why

NCDC's 2025 assessment review found that learners were over-rewarded for recalling reactions but under-rewarded for applying chemistry to real Ugandan contexts — water treatment in their districts, soap-making, fertilizer use on family farms. Lifting Application and naming Values explicitly is meant to bring the test back to the curriculum's original promise.

Classroom Example

An S3 student writes the correct equation for the neutralization of lake-water alkalinity but does not connect it to a community problem. Under the old weights they earned a B overall. Under the new weights, the missing Application drags the grade to C+ — and the absence of any reflection on responsible water use shows as a D for Values.

Common Pitfall

Do not simply re-mark old papers with new weights. The questions themselves were written to test Knowledge first. Re-design at least one Activity of Integration per term to give learners somewhere to demonstrate Application and Values.

07 — Design Philosophy

Quiet sophistication. The work shows itself only to those who look.

Restraint is respect. Most EdTech for African markets is loud — bright primaries, cartoon mascots, "Congratulations 🎉" modals. It's condescending. Sasa does the opposite. We use whitespace the way a watchmaker uses the dial.

The surprise is in the substrate. The user shouldn't say "wow, flashy." They should use Sasa for two weeks and slowly notice — the spring on a card dismiss is exactly right, the narrator pronounces Nakawa correctly, the export PDF has the school's motto in the footer.

Content is the hero. Chrome recedes. Liquid glass surfaces are reserved for things that float — the top bar, the audio player, modal sheets. Everything else is flat warm paper. Type does seventy percent of the visual work.

What we will never do

  • Confetti. Badges. Points. Leaderboards. The reward is the work itself.
  • Gradients longer than two stops or covering more than 30% of a screen.
  • Stock photos of African children. Map-of-Africa silhouettes. Kente patterns.
  • The word "engagement" anywhere in the UI.
  • "AI sparkle" icons. The AI is a colleague, not a magic trick.
  • Spinners. Skeletons only.
